  <h3 class="book-title text-[var(--book-title-1)] text-4xl">🙋 5.3 Flow Across Aquitards</h3>
  
  <hr class="linea-delgada bg-[var(--rayas)]"></hr>

  In some cases, the hydraulic conductivity of the aquitard is less restrictive, and some groundwater may flow between the overlying and underlying aquifers. Such a formation may be referred to as a leaky aquitard. In the vicinity of a recharge or discharge area, the groundwater in both unconfined and confined aquifers can have a significant vertical flow component even in the presence of an aquitard.

  Figure 27 shows that the direction of flow in the aquitard is dependent on the relative elevations of the water table and potentiometric surface. The flow direction is determined by the hydraulic gradient between the aquifers.

  <h3 class="book-title text-[var(--book-title-1)] text-3xl mb-4 mt-6">Problem 7 Solution</h3>

  <p>The step-by-step solution can be explored in the interactive exercise above. The key takeaways are:</p>

  <ul>
    <li><strong>Flow direction through an aquitard</strong> is determined by the <em>vertical hydraulic gradient</em> — the difference in hydraulic head between the aquifer and the overlying water body (lake or unconfined aquifer). Water always flows from higher head to lower head.</li>
    <li><strong>In a discharge area</strong>, the potentiometric head in the confined aquifer is higher than the water table (or lake level). This creates an <em>upward vertical gradient</em>, forcing water to leak upward through the low-K sediment into the lake.</li>
    <li><strong>In a recharge area</strong>, the water table (or lake level) is higher than the potentiometric head in the confined aquifer. This creates a <em>downward vertical gradient</em>, and water seeps downward through the sediment into the aquifer.</li>
    <li><strong>Darcy's Law applies even through low-K sediment.</strong> As long as there is a hydraulic head difference (Δh), there will be flow — the velocity simply scales with both the gradient (Δh/ΔL) and the hydraulic conductivity (K). Very low K means very slow flow, but not zero flow.</li>
    <li><strong>The vertical head profile</strong> shows constant head in the lake and in the aquifer (where flow is predominantly horizontal), with a linear gradient across the sediment (where flow is vertical). The slope of the profile line indicates the direction: upward slope = upward flow.</li>
  </ul>
