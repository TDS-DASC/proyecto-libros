<h3 class="book-title text-[var(--book-title-1)] text-4xl">
  🙋 Example Problem 12
</h3>
<p>
  This exercise integrates several key concepts about{" "}
  <strong>hydraulic head</strong> and <strong>groundwater flow</strong> in a
  system with an unconfined aquifer, a confined aquifer, and an aquitard
  separating them. The cross-section shows an upper sand unit, an upper clay
  aquitard, a lower sand unit, and a lower clay unit, with two monitoring wells.
</p>

<p>Before starting, recall these principles:</p>
<ul>
  <li>
    <strong>Potentiometric contours</strong> connect points of equal hydraulic
    head. In a homogeneous, isotropic aquifer with predominantly horizontal
    flow, they are approximately vertical lines.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>The potentiometric surface</strong> is the imaginary surface
    representing the level to which water would rise in a well open to the
    confined aquifer at any point.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Flow through an aquitard</strong> is vertical (either upward or
    downward), determined by comparing the head above vs. below the aquitard.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Darcy's law</strong> applies even through low-K aquitards: any
    hydraulic gradient produces some flow.
  </li>
</ul>

<h3 class="book-title text-[var(--book-title-1)] text-3xl mb-4 mt-6">
  Problem 12 Solution
</h3>

<p>
  The step-by-step solution can be explored in the interactive exercise above.
  Key takeaways:
</p>

<ul>
  <li>
    <strong>Potentiometric contours (Part a)</strong> in the lower sand unit are
    evenly spaced vertical lines based on linear interpolation of head values
    between the wells — head increases from left (36 m at Well 1) to right (40 m
    at Well 2), so contours are labeled 36, 37, 38, 39, 40 m from left to right.
    In isotropic media, flow is orthogonal to equipotential lines, confirming
    predominantly horizontal flow in the confined aquifer.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>The potentiometric surface (Part b)</strong> is a planar surface (a
    straight line in cross-section) connecting the head elevations of the two
    wells screened in the lower confined aquifer (36 m at Well 1, 40 m at Well
    2). This surface lies above the water table at Well 2 (where head = 40 m >
    ~38 m water table), indicating upward flow through the aquitard.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>The flow direction through the upper clay unit (Part c)</strong> is{" "}
    <em>upward</em>. The potentiometric head in the lower sand at Well 2 is 40
    m, which is higher than the water table at that location (~38 m). Since
    water flows from higher to lower hydraulic head, flow through the aquitard
    is directed upward. This is an example of a discharge area where the
    confined aquifer contributes water to the unconfined aquifer.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>The expected vertical head profile (Part d)</strong> near Well 2
    shows: (1) a slight downward decrease in head through the unconfined aquifer
    (due to a non-horizontal, upward-flow component), (2) a sharp upward jump
    across the clay aquitard (because head must increase from ~38 m above to 40
    m below — flow is upward through the low-K unit), and (3) nearly constant
    head in the lower confined aquifer (where flow is predominantly horizontal).
    The head profile across the aquitard is a clear indicator of vertical
    leakage.
  </li>
</ul>

<h4 class="book-title text-[var(--book-title-1)] text-xl mt-6 mb-2">
  Conceptual Connections
</h4>
<ul>
  <li>
    Compare this exercise with Exercise 11, where flow through the aquitard is{" "}
    <em>downward</em>. The direction is reversed because in Ex. 11 the water
    table is higher than the potentiometric surface, while here it is lower.
  </li>
  <li>
    The sharp change in head across the aquitard (Part d) is a direct
    consequence of Darcy's law: the very low hydraulic conductivity of the clay
    requires a large hydraulic gradient to transmit even a small amount of flow.
  </li>
  <li>
    In both Exercise 11 and 12, the potentiometric surface and equipotential
    contours are determined by the same principles — they differ only in the
    specific head values and resulting flow directions.
  </li>
</ul>
